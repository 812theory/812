# UNIT — CHARACTER & PRODUCTION DESIGN NOTES
### Created: 2026-03-01

*Written during E2 development, when the Unit chase was first drafted. At that point the scene was thirty seconds: Unit locks onto something, sprints, Met follows winded, a corner with nothing there, Bangkok resumes. A quick break in the camera's rules — the first time the show lets something other than professional distance drive the frame. A plant.*

*By E4, the full vision landed. The Khao San Road Unit-cam scene is three minutes where the camera drops to eighteen inches, the palette goes warm instantly, and the show abandons every visual rule it spent four episodes building — because a dog found the real Bangkok and the rules couldn't hold that. The design spec in this document describes E4, not E2. It was written toward a promise that E4 kept.*

*Read E2 for the seed. Read E4 for what it became.*

---

## UNIT AS CHARACTER, NOT PROP

Unit is a grief-witness maintaining vigil. After E2's chase scene, every time Unit watches the corner, the audience isn't seeing "cute dog does weird thing." They're seeing a creature who remembers the daughter Met's brain erased, standing watch because the game never ended.

- E1: Unit watches the corner. No explanation. Planted with zero context.
- E2: Unit chases Noi's ghost through Bangkok. Sound disappears. Payoff with zero dialogue.
- After E2: corner-watching retroactively becomes the most emotionally loaded recurring image in the show.

Plant-and-payoff executed entirely through behavior and production design. No exposition. No dialogue.

---

## UNIT-CAM — DISTINCT PERCEPTUAL MODE

Unit-cam is NOT a POV gimmick. It is the show presenting an unfiltered consciousness — what reality looks like when perception isn't reduced by trauma, duty, data, or theory.

**Visual treatment:**
- Warmer color palette than Met's Bangkok (which the audience has accepted as "normal")
- More saturated — not stylized, MORE REAL
- Low camera position (dog-height) — city is enormous, overwhelming, alive
- Distinct from all human camera modes (Met-cam follows eyes, Chloe-cam follows screens, stationary camera witnesses)

**Sound treatment:**
- Fuller mix — more simultaneous layers than any other scene in the show
- Every sound is distinct: tokay, vendor calls, motorbikes, temple bells, construction — not cacophony, ORCHESTRA
- The sound is warmer. Not processed differently — RECEIVED differently. Unit hears love in the noise.

**The foundational principle:** Unit's relationship to the world IS warmth. His Bangkok is louder, more colorful, more alive — not because the city is different but because Unit isn't subtracting anything from it. This is HIS Bangkok. HIS Met. HIS Noi.

**Effect on audience:** Five seconds of Unit-cam and the audience realizes what they thought was the full Bangkok was already reduced. They were inside Met's filter and didn't know it. When Unit-cam ends and we return to Met's version, the audience FEELS the loss. They want Unit's Bangkok back. That wanting is the show teaching them what Met's grief costs.

**For Thai audiences:** Unit-cam is the show's love letter to Bangkok. Full. Alive. Beauty in the chaos. The Thai viewer who feels they've lost something to westernization will feel this without naming it. Simple in expression, deep on the inside. That's Thai culture. The show sees it.

---

## UNIT'S IMMUNITY TO THE STATIC

The visual bible states: Unit never flickers, never desaturates, never simplifies.

**Why:** The Static operates on filtered perception. It degrades what's being reduced. Unit's perception is unfiltered — love without reduction. The Static can't touch what isn't being filtered.

Every other character filters reality through something:
- Met: trauma (reduction to solvable variables)
- Chloe: data (reduction to measurable patterns)
- Ice: duty (reduction to manageable tasks)
- Steve: theory (reduction to explainable frameworks)

Unit filters through nothing. The signal comes in full. Love is the mechanism, not the sentiment.

## THE E2 CHASE SCENE — DESIGN SPEC

**Setup:** Morning walk with Met. Normal Bangkok cacophony. Unit locks on a DIRECTION. Takes off running.

**Camera:** Breaks from all established modes. GoPro-on-tail or Unit-eye POV. First non-human perspective in the show. "Here's what it looks like when perception isn't filtered through trauma."

**Unit looks back at Met — twice:**
- First look: "are you coming?" Met has barely started moving.
- Second look: locked back on target. Met doesn't matter. Hierarchy revealed — Unit's loyalty to Met is real, but Unit's loyalty to Noi is OLDER.

**Sound design during the run:**
- Bangkok's layers strip away as Unit runs (same degradation as E5 memory, but on a living street)
- One layer remains: a child's laugh. Far away. Wrong frequency. Not quite real.
- Ghost-frequency of "catch me Unit!" — not literal words. Emotional residue. A game that never ended.

**The stop:** Corner of a street. Nothing there. Unit stares at empty space. Almost-silence. Child's laugh fading.

**Bangkok SLAMS back.** Full volume. Every layer at once. Unit flinches.

**Unit looks back — once, twice — then walks back calmly.** Not the first time. Patience, not resignation. The game isn't over.

**Met catches up, winded:** "What the HELL was that?" Unit is already walking normally.

The audience files it as the first time the show scared them.

---

## UNIT'S SEASON ARC

- E1: Watches the corner. No explanation.
- E2: The chase. Sound disappears. Corner-watching gains weight.
- E3-E4: Corner-watching continues. Audience reads it as vigil.
- E5: Present during Pai memory crack. Only witness who doesn't need the wall to come down — never had one.
- E6-E7: Behavior escalates as Static worsens. Not because Unit is affected — because what Unit is watching for is harder to reach.
- E8: "Unit's corner is empty." The vigil ends. The absence of the recurring image is the payoff.
