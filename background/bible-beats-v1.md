## THE ENGINE

Each episode follows the same structural pattern:

**A new case arrives.** A building that shouldn't be standing. An insurance claim that doesn't add up. A structural anomaly that defies engineering. Met investigates. Met SOLVES it — genuinely, brilliantly. The case closes. Everyone is satisfied.

**Except.** Chloe looks at the same data and sees what Met subtracted. His solution is technically correct but rationally stretched over something irrational. He solved the math. She's looking at the physics. Both right. Only one looking at the actual problem.

**The complete-but-incomplete pattern:** The audience gets a satisfying investigation AND an unsatisfying resolution. Met closes the case. Chloe opens it back up. Same data, different conclusion. Met isn't wrong — Met is incomplete. His reduction leaves a remainder. Chloe IS the remainder.

This is the Severance parallel: the numbers make sense inside the room. They mean nothing outside it. Met's case reports make sense inside his framework. Outside that framework, space itself is wrong.

## THE RULES OF THE WORLD

**The Coherence Field.** Bangkok has a pulse. At 8:12 every morning, for exactly one second, the city goes silent. Every city sound — traffic, construction, vendors, dogs, music — stops. Then resumes. No one notices except Met. He doesn't know why he notices. He doesn't talk about it. It's the first thing the audience sees (cold open, Episode 1) and the last thing they understand (finale).

**Static.** The wrongness in the world has a presence. Not a character. Not a monster. A CONDITION. Static manifests in the background: a building sign that reads "SERVICES" where no sign should be, a cooking show where pasta is made from pasta, a 7-11 chime that plays HELLO WELCOME HELLO instead of the standard jingle. Static is always present. The camera doesn't linger on it. The audience catches it or they don't.

**The Buildings.** Met's cases are structural investigations, but the buildings are also metaphors. Each building that shouldn't stand represents something being held up by forces that shouldn't exist. As the series progresses, the connection between Met's unresolved grief and the structural impossibilities becomes clearer — not through exposition, but through pattern.

**Unit.** Met's elderly golden retriever. Unit perceives things Met can't or won't — head-tilts at empty corners, stares at spaces where nothing is, follows sounds only he can hear. Unit is not a plot device. Unit is a DIAGNOSTIC INSTRUMENT. If Unit reacts to something, it's real. If Unit ignores it, it's Met's imagination. The audience learns this rule without being told.

**The Head Tilt.** A recurring visual motif. Same camera angle, same lens, same beat of hesitation before the head moves. Three times in Episode 1: Unit at the TV, Met at the 7-11, Met hearing "Find me, Daddy" in the dream. Recurs throughout series. Non-negotiable consistency — same setup every time. Direction-level flagged.

## 8-EPISODE ARC

### Episode 1: "Load-Bearing"
The pilot. Cold open at 8:12. Met's world established. First case (Khlong Toei building). Chloe arrives. Investigation engine demonstrated. Dream sequence: the shaving mirror, the smile, "Find me Daddy," the grey hallway. Unit watches the corner. The audience has questions about everything.

### Episode 2: "Sathorn"
Second case deepens the engine. The audience sees the pattern: Met solves, Chloe finds the remainder. Ice's filing system gets its first real test. The 8:12 moment happens again — this time the audience is WATCHING for it.

### Episode 3: "Survey Lines"
Chloe's data mapping reveals geographic convergence across Met's old cases. The twin sister enters (phone call in Ep1 was the seed). Met and Chloe's working relationship shifts — she stops asking permission to look at closed files.

### Episode 4: "Foundation"
Mid-season turn. A case hits close to home — the building is near where Noi disappeared. Met's professional detachment cracks. Unit's behavior escalates. Ice makes a decision about the second filing cabinet.

### Episode 5: "Shear Wall"
Met tries to close the Noi investigation his way — with math, with logic, with engineering. It doesn't work. The remainder is too big. Chloe and Met's first real conflict — she won't let him reduce this one.

### Episode 6: "Dead Load"
The weight of everything Met has been carrying. Flashback structure — the audience finally sees the day Noi disappeared. Not what they expected. Unit was there.

### Episode 7: "Resonance"
Everything converges. The buildings. The silence. The filing cabinets. Chloe's data. The 8:12 moment. Met has to stop solving and start listening.

### Episode 8: "8:12"
The answer was never in the buildings. The answer was in the pause. What the city has been trying to tell him. What Unit always knew. Resolution that is emotionally complete and mythologically open — the door stays cracked for the audience.

**Post-credits:** Seeds expansion without requiring it. The show is complete. The world isn't.

## THEMATIC ARCHITECTURE

**The show is about reduction.** Met is a genius because he reduces complex systems to solvable equations. This is also his fatal flaw. He reduced his grief to a case file. He reduced his daughter to an unsolved problem. He reduced Bangkok to a dataset. The show asks: what happens when you stop subtracting the things that don't fit?

**Grief as structural failure.** A building that shouldn't stand is a person who shouldn't function. Met functions. He works. He solves. He eats. He walks his dog. He is structurally sound. And something fundamental is wrong with the foundation that no one can see — except a dog and a woman with a spreadsheet.

**Found family through parallel damage.** Met, Chloe, Ice, and Unit don't bond. They recognize each other. Each is carrying something they've reduced to manageable size. The show is about what happens when the reductions stop holding.
