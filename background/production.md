## BANGKOK: WHY IT HAS TO BE HERE

Bangkok is not the setting. Bangkok is the diagnostic toolkit.

When the Static eats the city's specificity, the audience has to feel the loss of REAL things. The 7-11 chime that every expat has heard four hundred times. The temple chanting that carries two blocks through humid air. The soi layout that defies western urban planning. The Sukhumvit chaos that makes no sense until you stop applying western frameworks. TIT — This Is Thailand — isn't a joke. It's the show's thesis. You can't lose what was never specific.

### Production Reality vs Studio Perception
Studios will ask "can we shoot Australia or Atlanta?" The answer is no. This is a studio perception issue, not a production reality issue.

Bangkok has a mature film production infrastructure, government incentive programs for international productions, and this project has existing relationships with Thai tourism authorities (TTT). The Thai government will support this project — it's jobs, real Thailand on screen, pure tourism budget. Permits will flow. Locations will be secured. Sukhumvit could be closed for the first time ever for the right production.

There are rules, then there are RULES in Thailand. The bible needs to communicate this confidence without spelling it out.

### Key Location Requirements

**Met's Office** — Small, unglamorous. Real Sukhumvit side-street. Two filing cabinets at Ice's desk. The kind of place you'd walk past a hundred times.

**The 8:12 Path** — Met's walking route. Must pass a real 7-11 (chime calibration). Traffic, construction, tuk-tuks, vendors — the choreography of BKK's morning chaos bending around one man.

**Khlong Toei Building** — Episode 1 investigation. Real building, real neighborhood. Working-class Bangkok, not tourist Bangkok. The building needs to feel like it shouldn't be standing — age, cracks, character.

**Met's Bungalow** — Simple. Not a Thai house, not an expat apartment. A bungalow — the word matters. Ordered. Sterile. The absence of life masquerading as minimalism.

**7-11** — Bangkok's one constant. Must be a REAL 7-11 with the real door chime. The chime arc (8 episodes) requires the authentic sound as baseline.

**Margarita Storm** — Corner of Soi 13, Sukhumvit Road. 24-hour Tex-Mex. Greg the owner. Pool tables upstairs. This is a real place. It has to be THE real place or a set built to match it. The Guardians moment happens here.

**Sathorn Unique (Ghost Tower)** — The abandoned skyscraper where Met Authors. Real landmark. Permission negotiation required but this is exactly the kind of thing Thai authorities will support for the right production.

## ACTOR NOTES — WORKING CAST

**Met — Joel Edgerton.** Australian. Carries "man who lives far from where he started and stopped explaining why." His stillness makes the break devastating — the dream sequence needs a face that SHATTERS, and Edgerton holds so still that the shatter comes from nowhere. The Khlong Toei monologue comes out of him like he's reading a room — fast, flat, already bored by his own brilliance. The bottle flip as ritual, not bit.

**Ice — Urassaya Sperbund (Yaya).** Thailand's biggest crossover star. The range to play "dai yen yen ka ka kaaa" exhaustion AND the Gray Room steel. Thai audience takes her seriously. Western audience discovers her. The Harvey/Donna dynamic needs someone who owns a room by being the most competent person in it without being the loudest. Yaya has that quiet authority.

**Chloe — Claire Foy.** The softness isn't hidden — it's DEFENDED. Every warm impulse goes through a security checkpoint before it reaches her face. The apple-on-desk energy isn't confidence, it's armor. The stairwell phone call works because Foy crying isn't soft — it's a system failure. The geek reads differently on Foy than on most — she's a geek who never bothered being cool, not one who learned to be. The twin flicker on THAT face melts the subreddit.

**Steve — Jeff Bridges frequency.** "The Dude if the Dude had been doing cosmic IT support for centuries and the bowling alley was Bangkok." The grin is real. The ease is performed. The terror underneath is the truth. Whether literally Bridges or someone channeling that frequency, this is the Steve. The hands shaking when he buys them time in Pai needs to be tiny and devastating.

**Unit — Golden-Dox.** Real breed. Trained head-tilt (same angle, same timing, across 8 episodes). Must hold a mark, stare at a fixed point, convey "I remember something you don't" through body language. The hopping-over-ears comedy is natural, not performed. The dog trainer is as important as any actor on this show.

## VISUAL BIBLE

**See: 812-visual-bible.md** — Full visual bible with color architecture, camera philosophy, Static visual language, lighting arcs, head-tilt technical spec, sound design architecture, and reference compass.

Summary of key principles:
- The Static is PRODUCTION DESIGN, not VFX. Generic labels, simplified geometry, orderly extras.
- Color desaturates from edges inward. Met's radius stays vivid (coherence field). Unit NEVER desaturates.
- Each character gets their own camera treatment: Met-cam (spatial/eyes), Chloe-cam (temporal/screens), Ice-cam (observed by others), Steve-cam (off-center/wider lens).
- Sound degrades on a tracked arc: 15-20 layers (Ep1) to 5-7 (Ep7) back to full (Ep8).
- The head-tilt is technically specified: 50mm lens, below eye-level, 4.3-second total beat, 30% ambient dip.
