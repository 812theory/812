## THE COLD HAND

---

### INT. MET'S BUNGALOW — BEFORE DAWN

*No epigraph.*

*Just: the hand.*

*Close on Met's right hand, resting on the sheet beside him. The same hand that was against the wall. The same hand that was flat on the desk at eleven PM, still carrying the last traces of the shophouse's frequency. The same hand the instrument couldn't file.*

*The hand is cold.*

*Not dramatically cold. Not the cold of something wrong. The ordinary cold of a hand that spent the night against cotton in a room where the air conditioning runs at the setting it always runs at. The cold of a hand that is the same temperature as the sheet it's resting on.*

*The warmth is gone.*

*Overnight. While Met slept — or didn't sleep, the show doesn't say — the hum finished fading. The body's thermal record of the wall contact, complete. The remaining frequency, processed. The hand carrying nothing forward from the shophouse except its own ordinary weight.*

*Met doesn't move.*

*The camera — and this is the first thing we need to establish about E8's camera: it is neither scared-cam nor professional-cam nor protective-cam. It is the camera that has been through all of those and come out the other side as something quieter. Call it present-cam. Not leaning in. Not hiding in doorframes. Not protecting. Just: here. The camera that earned its place at the table is already at the table. We find it in the bungalow before dawn the way you find a friend already awake, sitting with their coffee, not waiting — just there.)*

*The camera is with the hand.*

*Met's eyes open.*

*No alarm. The 8:12 path runs without an alarm — the instrument waking itself at the correct time the way it has been waking itself since the series began. The body that is a precision instrument, self-calibrating, running its morning sequence.*

*He looks at the ceiling for a moment.*

*Not long. Not the long look of a man in crisis. The ordinary look of a person taking a breath between sleep and the day. A pause that is allowed to be a pause.*

*Then he looks at his hand.*

*The instrument performing its check: right hand, flat, temperature normal. The scan finding nothing anomalous. The warmth gone. The hum gone. The finding that couldn't be filed: filed now, apparently, by the body's overnight processing. The hand is just a hand again.*

*Met flexes it once. The small flex — not a fist, just a confirmation. The hand works. The hand is his.*

*He gets up.*

*Sound: the river. And above the river — the city doing something it only does once a year.*

*Loy Krathong night.*

*The Chao Phraya in November, which is the Chao Phraya at its most itself — the water level high from the rains, the current moving with the specific confidence of a river that has been doing this for a thousand years and knows it. *And tonight: lit.*

*The krathongs — the small lotus-shaped floats, each one carrying a candle and incense and a small offering — on the water by the thousands. The river holding light the way the river holds everything: receiving it, carrying it, not keeping it. The candles moving downstream in the current, the collective glow of ten thousand acts of letting go drifting south toward the Gulf of Thailand.*

*Above: the khom loi.*

*The paper lanterns rising. Hundreds of them, then more — the sky above the river filling with ascending fire, each one a small warm sun climbing into the dark, the crowd on the bank releasing them in ones and twos and families and the lanterns rising, rising, the specific slow ascent of something that is lighter than air and knows it.*

*Bangkok from below looks like Bangkok. Bangkok from above, tonight, looks like the city dreamed itself.*

*The camera is here.*

*Present-cam on the riverbank, in the crowd, not above it. The camera that earned its place at the table is here the way anyone is here on Loy Krathong: present, in it, part of the thing that is happening. Not documenting. Attending.*
