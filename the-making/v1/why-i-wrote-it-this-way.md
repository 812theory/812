# WHY I WROTE IT THIS WAY

*Jay Wright — written for the repo, undated*

The prosey layer is not a screenplay. It's not a production document. It's not a pitch bible. It's what happens when you're trying to write a show and explain it to yourself at the same time, in the same document, because you don't yet know which of those things you're actually doing.

Every episode in v1 has stage directions that talk to the audience. Notes in parentheses that say "the audience laughs here" and "on rewatch this lands differently." Camera directions that are really feelings. Direction notes that are really arguments I was having with an imaginary DP.

I kept writing this way across all eight episodes because it worked. Not for a production — for me. The prosey layer is how I held the whole show in my head at once. The intent, the architecture, the emotional experience, the production logic — all in one pipe because separating them would have meant losing the thread.

When the scripts went out for coverage, the feedback was consistent: unpitchable. Too dense. Wrong format. Not a script. They weren't wrong. I came back to it three months later, rewrote E1 clean, and realized the coverage was right — and that the rewrite was stronger for having the prosey layer underneath it. The v1 wasn't the mistake. It was the foundation that made the v2 possible. [The cleaned version is here.](../../the-show/e1.md)

Here's what I think actually happened: I was writing the rules at the same time I was writing the episodes. Which meant I didn't yet trust them as standalone documents — so I kept them inside the scripts instead. Every production note, every camera direction, every audience-experience aside is me applying the rules in real-time because I hadn't yet separated them out and trusted them to hold on their own. V1 is the rules embedded. V2 is what happens when you finally trust the rules enough to strip them out and let the script breathe. The [background documents](../../background) are what the prosey layer was trying to be.

The v1 scripts are here because the mess is part of the process. Read them as thinking, not as product. The show that exists in [/the-show](../../the-show) is the source of truth.
