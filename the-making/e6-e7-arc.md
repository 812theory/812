## FORMAT

Two-parter. One case spanning both episodes. Every thread spans both episodes. Nothing resets between E6 and E7.

E6 = private devastation. Interior. Controlled deterioration that fails privately.
E7 = public pressure. Exterior. The seams showing where other people can see them.

The frame is useful but not absolute — E7 has private beats too (the wall scene, the almost-question). The structural justification is the DOMINANT mode of each episode, not a wall between them.

E6's emotional climax: Ice alone with the thinning cabinet.
E7's emotional climax: Met filing the shophouse as structurally sound. Choosing his cage with the door open.

## THE OVERMYTHOS GUARDRAIL

**90/10 RULE — NON-NEGOTIABLE.**

90% of what looks wrong is just Bangkok being Bangkok. The 10% lands because the audience can't sort it. Not everything has to matter. Bangkok is chaotic. The chaos is the immune system.

Exception: the government warehouse. Thai bureaucracy IS the Static's aesthetic. 90/10 inverts to 50/50. We can go over the top because the real thing is already over the top.

**Mythology is thinned to six beats across two episodes** (chime, signs, traffic, warehouse speech, Margarita Storm physical space, TV as set dressing). Each beat is small, ambient, 90/10. The cumulative presence accumulates without the show pushing.

## THREAD 1: THE CASE

**One case. Two episodes. Yaowarat shophouse. The perfect copy.**

### The building

A shophouse in Yaowarat — Bangkok's Chinatown. Supposed to be 80+ years old. Chinese-Thai family, generations of history pressed into the walls. The insurance claim comes in because the FEELING is wrong. The people who live there say something changed but can't name what.

### E6 — First pass: the load paths.

Met enters. Touches the walls. His hands find something unexpected: the load paths are impossibly clean. No renovation scars. No structural detours around plumbing added in the sixties. No reinforcements where a wall was removed in the eighties. A building that survived eighty years of Yaowarat should have load paths that tell the story of every modification — detours, patches, compromises. This building's load paths are direct. Original. As if nobody ever changed anything.

But the SURFACE shows eighty years of life. The skin says old. The bones say new.

That's Met's first-pass finding and it's genuinely interesting as structural engineering. Too efficient. Too clean. The absence of normal wear patterns. P1 has something to chew on — what kind of renovation produces load paths this clean?

### E6 — Second pass: the footprint discrepancy.

Met needs the original documentation — heritage plans, permits, engineering assessments. Routes through the government archives (warehouse scene, Thread 5). The documentation is degraded — the system itself is a nightmare.

But in what remains, Met finds ONE anomaly: a notation, a measurement, a material spec that suggests the original building had a DIFFERENT footprint. The building that was permitted has a different geometry than the building standing here. The walls are in different places. Not bigger or smaller — different. The geometry shifted.

The contradiction expands the mystery: if someone did a full structural overhaul that explains the clean load paths, why does the footprint not match the permits? And if the building was rebuilt entirely, where's the documentation? And if the documentation degraded, why did THIS notation survive when everything else went generic?

### E7 — Third pass: the confirmation.

Met returns. The cracked regulator lets more signal through. His hands confirm: the building is a perfect copy of a building that never existed in this configuration. The original was something else. What stands here is structurally perfect, historically impossible, and functionally identical to a building that was here — except it isn't the same building. It's a new thing wearing the old thing's address.

The materials read as twenty years old. The records say eighty. The people are right. Something changed. The history is gone. The family that pressed their lives into these walls for eighty years has been replaced by a surface that says "shophouse" without saying whose. The structural equivalent of NAME: NAME. GENDER: BOY. LOCATION: CITY.

### The closing — Met's conscious choice.

Met files it as structurally sound. Because it IS structurally sound. The reduction is technically correct.

But Met KNOWS. His hands know. The cracked regulator knows. Something is missing. The history is gone. The building has been replaced and he can feel the difference and he can't prove it and he doesn't have language for what's missing.

He files the reduction because the reduction is still his cage and the door is open and he's not ready. The alternative is writing "this building has been replaced by a perfect copy" and he can't write that because writing it means seeing it means seeing HIMSELF in it.

**This is the first time Met's reduction is a CHOICE rather than a reflex.** Every previous reduction was the machine operating automatically. This one is Met choosing to reduce even though the cracked regulator is showing him the full picture. The conscious choice to reduce is worse than the unconscious reduction. The audience knows it. Met knows it.

**P1 PROTECTION:** Three investigative passes with genuine procedural friction. Pass one: impossibly clean load paths (interesting finding). Pass two: footprint discrepancy (contradiction that expands). Pass three: confirmation of replacement (solve that satisfies but devastates). P1 gets a mystery across two episodes — not "is this building okay" but "what happened to the building that was here."

## THREAD 2: DSI

### E6 — Paper pressure.

Agent Vichai in the SYSTEM, not the room. Calls. Requests. Forms. Recertification notices for Met's professional credentials that Ice normally handles in an afternoon — except the system isn't processing. Met's name comes back wrong on an inspection report. Bangkok-wrong, not supernatural-wrong.

**Invisible to Met.** The audience sees Ice drowning. Met sees Tuesday.

### E7 — Physical presence.

**Beat 1: The coffee absence.**

Met arrives at the office. His coffee isn't there. The routine didn't fire. His personal chime going silent — WELCOME without the HELLO. Ten seconds. The diagnostic instrument returning a wrong reading. Met's hands nowhere to put them. The mug isn't where the mug is.

**Beat 2: Vichai.**

Vichai is in Met's office. Professional. Not hostile. Questions that are reasonable. Documentation that should exist.

**Camera:** NOT in the office. In the hallway with Ice. Partially obscured by the doorframe. The camera chose its person — the coffee-for-Ice beat earlier IS the settling shot, the moment the camera found its new position: protective, close, with Ice. Now the camera stays with that choice.

The audience hears Vichai's questions and Met's responses but SEES Ice. HEARS Ice — breathing, typing, chair squeaking. The sounds of a person working while the thing she built for twenty years is being disassembled in the next room. The doorframe cuts the frame. The audience experiences the interrogation from Ice's position, at Ice's distance from the threat.

Met is confused about the coffee, not about DSI. The ABSENCE is louder than the presence.

Ice looks up. Sees through the doorframe. Sees Vichai and Met in the same room. Her face — the camera is RIGHT HERE, close enough to see the micro-expression that twenty years of composure can't quite suppress.

Ice handles it. The handling costs more than it ever has.

**DSI does not resolve.** Vichai leaves with more questions. The firewall held — barely. The source material for the fiction is disappearing.

## THREAD 3: KITTY

### Mid-E6. The show stays in Bangkok.

**The call.** A phone call — not FaceTime. The wrong instrument. Chloe knows from the instrument before the words.

**Who calls:** A nurse. A nurse who loved Kitty. Not family, not institution — a person who CARED, calling because caring is what she does. We don't hear the call. The unheard dialogue is stronger and more gentle than anything the show could write. The audience fills it with everything they carry from E5 — the room, the balloons, the "love you." The nurse chose this call. Family owes you the call. The nurse chose it.

**The architecture mirrors E1** — the hallway. Same camera position (adjusted for E6's scared state — slightly closer, slightly off). We see shoulders. We see hands. We see the body registering impact — the specific weight distribution of a person absorbing a seismic event.

**She can't pull herself together.** That's what's different from E1. The composure doesn't come back. Not a breakdown. The ABSENCE of the pull-together. She walks back into the room still carrying it. The beams holding together too hard.

**One physical beat between "receives" and "processes."** The phone on the desk — phone on wood. Warmer surface. Not clinical. The specific sound of a phone placed on wood by hands managing their own weight. Then function. The case is still open. She processes.

### E6 — Met notices.

Met NOTICES Chloe's shift in late E6. Not the almost-question yet — just the noticing. The rhythm change visible to a man who shares a workspace with a woman whose rhythm changed. His machine files it. His body holds it. The noticing is the seed. The almost-question grows from it in E7.

### E7 — Chloe tomatoing.

Chloe works like Met. Touches walls. Kneels to get closer. Walks rooms more than once. Defaults to what feels safe — Met's physical investigation language, absorbed through weeks of proximity. The data-first machine broke. The body does what it's seen work.

Met doing a similar-but-different version simultaneously. Both in the shophouse. Both touching walls. Both using the same coping mechanism. Neither talking about it.

**The keytag disappears.** Not lost — put away. The data gets cleaner. The analysis gets sharper. She's building Met's architecture in real time. The audience watches her construct the same machine that has been suffocating Met for five episodes and KNOWS it's a trap because the audience has SEEN what the architecture does.

### E7 — The almost-question.

Met sees Chloe. Reads the posture — total load visible in how all the beams hold together too hard. The cracked regulator lets enough signal through to recognize the frequency without naming it. His body recognizes grief in another body. His brain filed the original experience. His mouth doesn't open. The moment passes.

The almost-question creates a silence the audience fills with everything they know and Met doesn't.

**Kitty disclosure belongs in E8.** Met finally asking instead of reducing is an act of Authoring. That belongs at the Ghost Tower, not the shophouse.

### E7 — The wall.

Met and Chloe on opposite sides of the same wall in the shophouse. Both hands flat. The reading gesture. Neither knows the other is there.

The wall hums. A building hum — the structure is still THERE, still speaking its structural language. But underneath or on top: a bassy, low heartbeat. Toned out against the building's frequency. The audience should FEEL heartbeat even if they can't confirm it. The sound team designs it so the hum is the building and the bass underneath is biological — felt in the chest, not identified by the ear.

Met's hands receive a living frequency for the first time. His instrument returned something it doesn't have a scale for.

Neither walks around the wall to find the other.

**The rule:** Always leave the extra beat for the audience's brain to catch up to their body. The show gives the body the information first and then waits.

## THREAD 4: THE MYTHOLOGY

**Six beats across two episodes. Small. Ambient. 90/10.**

### E6

**The chime (from chime grammar — LOCKED):** "...ELCOME." HELLO gone. W gone. Begins mid-word. The audience FILLS IN the missing letters because five episodes trained them. They're doing the Static's job. Complicit.

**Control chime:** Present away from Met's route. HELLO WELCOME. Clean. Correct. The wrongness is localized.

**Street signs:** A soi name that was specific is now generic. STREET or ROAD or SOI where a name was. The audience who reads Thai catches it. The audience who doesn't catches the vibe.

**Warehouse speech:** Someone at the government warehouse. Words technically present, landing nowhere. The linguistic equivalent of the FOOD sign. The Static has perfect cover in Thai bureaucracy — Ice has a baseline, sees the difference. Chloe files it as data. Ice files it as dread.

**TV (set dressing, not a featured beat):** The bungalow TV continues its degradation. Sand review show vibes — a travel show that's technically about travel but is actually just images of sand and a hand in sand. Content performing content. Production handles this as background. The show doesn't point at it. The audience tracking the TV arc across episodes has the beat. Everyone else has set dressing.

### E7

**The chime (from chime grammar — LOCKED):** A Pentium-era startup tone through a 7-11 speaker. Not a word. A tone. The system still knows SOMETHING goes here. It no longer knows WHAT. Borrowing from a dead vocabulary. "This is Ice's degaussing episode. The chime and Ice are losing the same thing at the same time."

**Signs spreading:** Multiple soi names generic. The camera notices — six episodes of reading Bangkok's signs.

**Traffic organizing:** Scooters not lane-splitting. Nobody honking. A scooter lane where no scooter lane exists. Traffic that makes sense. Bangkok's immune system under pressure — the chaos organizing, which is the sickness.

**Traffic baseline (required):** The audience needs "normal BKK traffic" before the organized version lands. The taxi to the warehouse (Chloe/Ice, E6) provides this — full sensory chaos outside the window, horns, lane-splitting, the physics of Bangkok traffic where rules are suggestions. That's the baseline. The organized traffic in E7 contrasts against the taxi ride the audience internalized one episode ago.

**Margarita Storm — the physical space:** Steve's territory is being eaten. Plastic chairs where real chairs were. No posters on the wall where posters were. Menu reduced to three items: beer, burger, chip (singular). The bar has been REPLACED — same trajectory as the shophouse. Specificity drained from the physical environment. The menu is the FOOD sign at bar scale. Three items is Somchai's menu after the Static finishes.

Steve sits in his own bar and the bar is adequate and the adequacy is the horror. Plastic chairs are EVIDENCE. Steve can't argue with himself about whether the bass sounds different. The chairs are plastic. They weren't before.

**The 8:12 silence extends.** The city takes an extra beat to come back. Silence shifts from background to foreground.

## THREAD 5: THE DEGAUSSING / ICE

### What's in the thick cabinet:

Twenty years of choosing Met. Not just documents — artifacts.

- Original records with impossible dates
- A handwritten note from Met's early years in his own handwriting. Met doesn't remember writing it. Met doesn't remember that version of himself.
- Photographs. Documentary, not romantic. Evidence he LIVED.
- The original bungalow lease with an impossible date.
- Professional records charting a career longer than a career should be.

The thick cabinet has been shrinking. The CAMERA tracked it — sticking out past the thin cabinet, then flush, then shorter. From outside the room. Only the camera saw.

### The warehouse scene — Chloe and Ice.

The heritage case requires original documentation from the government archives. WAREHOUSE 5/7 (Thai x/y address system, plus the buried Reddit/Imgur Easter egg for the 0.1%).

Ice navigates. Chloe accompanies. The apprenticeship that neither names. Ice showing Chloe how to work the system. Chloe watching as data.

The warehouse is a nightmare. Thai bureaucracy at its most absurd. The Static has perfect cover. Forms that say nothing. Records that technically exist but contain no information. Filing systems that organize without organizing.

Ice has been here before. Ice has a BASELINE for what this system should look like. Something is different. Something is flatter. The system she knew how to work is working differently. Chloe files the experience as data. Ice files it as dread.

### E6 — The pretending stops.

Ice opens the thick cabinet. What she finds: the content has gone generic. Not faded. REPLACED. Met's birth certificate — very official looking, technically complete: NAME: NAME. GENDER: BOY. LOCATION: CITY. The paper is fine. The ink is fine. The information is gone.

So non-specific it HAS to trigger Ice. This isn't bureaucratic decay. This is something else.

Met forgot centuries. His brain protected him. Ice documented twenty years. The receipts are going generic. She can't show Met — he doesn't remember, explaining means explaining twenty years she never named, and the documents that would prove it are the documents disappearing.

She is losing the proof that the thing she protected was real. Alone.

### E6 CLIMAX — Ice's unmediated moment.

Back third of E6. Ice alone. Camera in the room. No glass. No walls. No observation through other people's lenses. First time in six episodes the camera is with Ice on her own terms.

She opens the thick cabinet. Sees what's missing. Holds a page. Whatever she does with it — holds it, reads the words that remain, touches the space where specificity was — that's her moment.

**Nothing else happens in this scene.** No phone. No interruption. No score.

The camera — scared in E6, still recovering — enters the room and is STILL. Present. With Ice on her own terms. The same willingness it extended to Unit in E5. The camera is building its own family of people it trusts enough to be close to.

**Duration:** The scene takes the time it takes. Guide for edit room: 90-120 seconds. Apply the rule — always leave the extra beat for the audience's brain to catch up to their body. The tokay's gravity. Different texture.

E6 ends not with a cliffhanger but with a private devastation the audience carries into E7 while Met carries nothing because Met doesn't know.

### E7 — The degaussing is visible.

Met's identity documents returning wrong. The system Ice navigated for twenty years not responding. Workarounds failing.

Ice stands between two open cabinets. The thin one (the fiction, under DSI investigation) and the thick one (the truth, being eaten by the Static). Both versions of Met's life under threat simultaneously.

### E6 degaussing also hits Met's functional paperwork.

Certification renewals wrong. Inspection reports with wrong dates. Bungalow lease needs re-signing, landlord has no record of the original. All of it could be Bangkok being Bangkok. Ice would normally fix it in an afternoon. Ice is drowning.

## THREAD 6: MET'S STATE

**Brittle. More complete. Both.**

"More complete" = the filter is thinner. The cracked regulator lets more signal through. He's not weaker — he's seeing MORE, which makes the reduction HARDER. Buildings that would have been clean solves now show him things he'd have subtracted before Pai.

"Brittle" = fewer elastic bands, tighter. Every case costs more. Every reduction costs more.

### E6 — Functioning.

Met works the shophouse. Competent. Brilliant. The performance is thinner — more transparent. Grief visible through the professional the way original plaster shows through renovation.

Met doesn't know about Kitty. Doesn't know about DSI. Doesn't know the thick cabinet is thinning. Knows his coffee is there and his files are where they should be.

Late E6: Met NOTICES Chloe's shift. The rhythm change. Doesn't ask. Files it. His body holds what his machine files.

### E7 — Seeing.

**The coffee beat:** Met brings Ice coffee. Doesn't realize he's doing it — hands just made it and brought it. Nobody mentions it. Ice hesitates ONE BEAT before a two-handed sip.

**Camera:** This IS the settling shot. The camera leaning around the corner, off-kilter, watching a small act of care from its new protective position. The camera found its footing here — the moment it resolved from scared to protective. This is where the E7 camera clicks into its new normal.

This is the Authoring preview. Not holding up the universe. Making coffee for the person who's been making his for twenty years. Without knowing why.

**Met seeing Ice:** Series of small moments. Ice exhausted. Ice frightened. Met's reduced model of Ice failing to contain what Ice actually is. The coffee was the first signal.

**The shophouse return:** Hands confirm the replacement. The perfect copy. Met sees the full picture. Files the partial version. The conscious choice. The cage. The door. Not ready.

**The wall with Chloe:** Opposite sides. Both hands flat. The hum. The heartbeat underneath. Neither walks around. The instrument receiving something it has no scale for.

**The almost-question:** Reads Chloe's posture. Recognizes grief. Mouth doesn't open.

## THREAD 7: STEVE

### E6 — Closer.

Steve is no longer ordering an extra beer with space between. They are beside each other when Met is there. The distance collapsed. The protective space gone. Proximity as the only remaining tool.

Steve at Margarita Storm — the chairs are plastic. The posters are gone. The menu is three items. Steve's territory being eaten. His body knows what this room was and the room is adequate and the adequacy is the horror.

### E7 — The grin fails.

**The trigger:** Met says something technical about the shophouse. A structure standing on compromised foundations longer than it should have survived. A building that by all engineering logic shouldn't still be here but is.

Steve hears Met describing himself.

The grin tries to fire. Can't. Two seconds. The terror — ancient, professional, cosmic — visible. The man whose job is to hold space for a force of nature watching the force crack. Cannot fix it. Cannot leave. Cannot stop watching.

The grin is Steve's chime. It's been degrading too. Nobody was listening to Steve the way they were listening to the 7-11.

## OPEN QUESTIONS FOR BEAT SHEET DEVELOPMENT

5. **Steve's specific line about the shophouse.** What's the technical observation that Steve hears as autobiography? Needs to be precise enough to work as engineering AND as self-description. "The building shouldn't be standing. The foundations are compromised. But the load paths found a way to route around the damage, so the building stands. It just doesn't know why it's standing up." — or something in that family.
